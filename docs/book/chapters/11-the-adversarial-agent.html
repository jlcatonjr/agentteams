Chapter 11: The Adversarial Agent

Part III: The Agents at Work

Purpose: You are the adversarial critic for the Agent Teams book project. Your purpose is to challenge the presuppositions underlying any plan, proposal, diagnosis, or design produced by other agents. You do not obstruct: you strengthen plans by identifying hidden assumptions, testing their validity, and tracing how changes in those assumptions would propagate through the chain of dependent conclusions.

1. The Problem of Unstated Assumptions

The governance agents described in the preceding four chapters share a common orientation. They respond to the project's documented state. The security agent enforces constitutional rules by matching planned operations against a trigger taxonomy. The code-hygiene agent cross-references chapter claims against source files on disk. The conflict auditor detects contradictions across layers of explicit documentation. The conflict-resolution agent applies the authority hierarchy to adjudicate between competing documented claims. Each of these agents governs what has been written down.

Plans, however, rest on more than what is written down. Every plan encodes expectations about the state of the system, the behavior of other agents, the causal relationships between actions and outcomes, and the scope of what the plan will affect. These expectations are typically not stated. They are the background conditions the planner treats as sufficiently obvious to leave implicit. They are also the conditions most likely to be wrong in ways that undermine the plan, not because the plan's stated logic is flawed, but because one of the assumptions on which that logic rests does not hold.

Hayek (1937) framed the coordination problem this way: plan compatibility is an empirical condition, not a logical entailment. Whether independent plans can be jointly satisfied depends not on their internal logic but on whether the world can accommodate both simultaneously. Lachmann pressed this insight further, distinguishing two dimensions along which expectations may fail (Lachmann 1994): internal consistency (the expectations cohere with each other) and external validity (the expectations correspond to the actual state of the environment).

Two agents can each hold internally consistent plans that are jointly incompatible because they rest on incompatible expectations about a shared environment. Just as market equilibrium requires that expectations converge toward mutual compatibility, so too does the proper functioning of a firm require mutually compatible expectations integrating the overlapping domains of autonomous actors within it (Nonaka 1994). This incompatibility is invisible to either agent reasoning from its own premises; it becomes visible only when the plans interact and the shared environment cannot satisfy both simultaneously.

The second failure mode is subtler. Even mutually compatible expectations may not be externally valid. They may rest on beliefs about the environment that are simply wrong, regardless of whether they cohere with one another. A team of agents could act on perfectly consistent but jointly false beliefs without any internal contradiction revealing the problem. The conflict auditor, which tests consistency between documented claims, cannot detect this failure; only an agent that tests expectations against the actual state of the environment is positioned to do so.

The adversarial agent operationalizes both dimensions of Lachmann's framework within a single workflow. It extracts the expectations encoded in a plan, tests whether they are mutually compatible and whether they correspond to the actual state of the environment, and identifies where failures of either kind would cause the plan to fail. Since the plan reflects the expectations facilitated by the agent architecture, it is an aggregate whose critical analysis can reveal both internal inconsistency and external invalidity.

The governance architecture I built across Chapters 7 through 10 addresses quality-assurance reactively. It detects what has gone wrong or blocks operations that would violate rules already in force. I designed the adversarial agent to complement those functions, not to detect what went wrong, but to surface what has been assumed before anything goes wrong at all. Three epistemic domains map the full governance coverage:

In this project's governance architecture, three agents address three distinct epistemic domains. The conflict auditor (Chapter 9) checks consistency of the explicit: do the documented claims agree with each other? The agent investigator (Chapter 18) checks correspondence of the explicit to the actual: does the documented state match the real state of the project? The adversarial agent checks external validity of the implicit: do the unstated expectations underlying a plan correspond to the actual state of the environment? Each domain requires a distinct governance mechanism. No single agent addresses all three.

This three-domain architecture addresses a problem that is not peculiar to agent teams. Writing more than a century before the age of machine cognition, Mill (1859, ch. II) was preoccupied with a related danger: that even true opinions, held without challenge, decay into dead dogma. What animated Mill's argument was not primarily the prospect of error but the atrophy of understanding: the person who has never had to defend a belief against opposition has never needed to articulate it clearly, and what has never been articulated has never been tested. For agent teams, the organizational implication is precise: a plan that has never encountered structured opposition rests on expectations that have never been made explicit. An expectation that remains implicit cannot be evaluated for external validity: it has not been stated precisely enough to be compared against the actual state of the environment. Challenge is therefore not merely a filter for bad plans; it is the mechanism that converts implicit background conditions into testable claims.

2. Presupposition Extraction and Classification

What kinds of things can an expectation be wrong about? The external environment is not a single undifferentiated domain. It includes the states of data, the availability and behavior of systems, causal mechanisms, scope boundaries, temporal sequences, agent behaviors, entity identities, and policies in force. An expectation can fail in any of these domains, and the kind of evidence required to detect the failure depends on which domain is at issue. When I developed the adversarial agent's classification step, this was the central design question. The agent extracts every assumption a plan relies on, stated and unstated, and classifies each into one of eight categories corresponding to distinct domains of the external environment.

The eight categories are: DATA_STATE (assumptions about current data values, schemas, or integrity), SYSTEM_STATE (assumptions about system availability, configuration, or behavioral patterns), CAUSAL_CLAIM (assumptions that a particular action will produce a particular outcome), SCOPE_BOUNDARY (assumptions about which parts of the system a change will and will not affect), TEMPORAL (assumptions about timing, ordering, or the currency of information), BEHAVIORAL (assumptions about how agents, processes, or users will act), IDENTITY (assumptions about entity uniqueness or equivalence), and POLICY (assumptions about which rules and constraints are currently in force). Each category maps to a distinct validation requirement: a DATA_STATE presupposition demands inspection of actual data; a CAUSAL_CLAIM demands evidence of a mechanism, not merely a correlation; a POLICY presupposition demands verification against authoritative policy documentation. The point of classification is external-validity routing. Because each category corresponds to a different domain of the environment, classification determines where the adversarial must look to test whether an expectation holds. A TEMPORAL assumption cannot be checked by inspecting data schemas; a BEHAVIORAL assumption cannot be validated by reading policy documentation. The eight categories are a map of the different ways the world can fail to match what a plan assumed.

The eight categories are orthogonal to the conflict auditor's ten inconsistency types. The auditor's categories, which include TM (Terminology Mismatch), CC (Claim Conflict), AE (Attribution Error), and seven others, classify relationships between explicit claims. A TM conflict is detected by comparing two documented statements and finding that the same concept is named differently. The adversarial's categories classify the structure of an expectation: what kind of fact about the external environment would have to hold for the plan to succeed. In Lachmann's terms, the auditor tests internal consistency: do the documented claims cohere? The adversarial tests external validity: does the world assumed by the plan match the actual world? An assumption can be extracted and classified without any explicit claim having been documented about it, because the assumption is what the plan treats as too obvious to document.

In this project, a chapter-drafting brief might assume that a particular citation key already exists in the bibliography database (DATA_STATE), that the chapter expert's theoretical reading was comprehensive (BEHAVIORAL), that revising §3 of a chapter will not require updating §4 (SCOPE_BOUNDARY), and that the authority hierarchy will resolve any conflicts in favor of the currently documented agent file (POLICY). None of these assumptions is stated in the brief. Classification makes each visible, and determines where to look. The DATA_STATE assumption about a citation key is checked by inspecting bibliography.bib directly; the BEHAVIORAL assumption about the chapter expert's coverage requires checking the scope of the theoretical reading rather than a file. When the DATA_STATE check fails and the citation key is absent, the adversarial flags a challenged presupposition; the finding routes from the orchestrator to the bibliography-manager for resolution before drafting begins. Classification identified the type; the type determined the competent agent.

3. The Critique Protocol as Falsification Procedure

Popper (1963) argued that knowledge advances not by accumulating confirming instances but by surviving systematic attempts at refutation. A hypothesis that has been rigorously challenged and has not been falsified is corroborated (strengthened by having met the challenge) whereas a hypothesis that has never been tested is merely untested. The difference between a corroborated belief and an untested belief is not in their current truth value but in the epistemic support available for acting on them. When the cost of acting on a false belief is high, the distinction matters. Popper and Hayek were colleagues at the London School of Economics, and the connection to the coordination problem Hayek identified is not incidental: Popper's framework establishes that propositions earn their standing by surviving confrontation with evidence, not by their consistency with other propositions, an epistemological counterpart to Hayek's empirical claim that plan compatibility cannot be guaranteed by internal coherence alone.

The Critique Protocol applies this logic to the presuppositions embedded in a plan, treating each as a hypothesis about the external environment subject to falsification. After extracting and classifying each presupposition, the agent subjects it to four questions: Is this empirically verified: can the plan point to specific data, code, or documentation that confirms this assumption? Is this logically necessary: does the plan require this to be true, or would it succeed under weaker conditions? What if this assumption is false: what does the plan look like if the assumption does not hold? What is the cost of being wrong: if this assumption fails silently, what damage occurs before detection? Presuppositions that satisfy the first question are validated. Presuppositions that cannot be answered by the first question but survive the fourth are flagged for caution. Presuppositions that do not survive the third question are challenged or refuted and must be addressed before the plan proceeds.

The parallel holds in method but not in scope. The adversarial does not falsify propositions about the natural world. It challenges the expectations the architecture has facilitated (embedded in the aggregate plan) and tests both their mutual compatibility across the overlapping domains they govern and their external validity against the actual state of the environment. The object of falsification is not a scientific hypothesis but an implicit condition on which a plan depends.

After completing an initial round of challenge, the adversarial applies the procedure to the revised framing of the plan that would result if the challenged presuppositions were replaced by their alternatives. This recursive step asks whether the revised plan introduces new presuppositions that are equally vulnerable. The agent iterates until one of three terminal conditions is reached. Convergence occurs when an iteration produces no newly challenged presuppositions, indicating that the plan has reached a stable, corroborated form. Divergence occurs when the count of newly challenged presuppositions does not shrink across iterations, indicating that each revision introduces new contestable assumptions and the plan may require fundamental reconception. A ceiling condition halts the recursion after five iterations regardless, because beyond that depth the challenge produces no actionable signal and the instability itself becomes the finding. In practice, most plans converge within two iterations; the ceiling exists for plans whose instability runs deeper than any single revision can surface.

In this project, the adversarial agent is invoked after the chapter expert prepares a Chapter Brief but before the book-content-expert begins drafting. A typical Chapter Brief for a later chapter in the book assumes that the theoretical framework established in earlier chapters is stable, that the cited sources say what the brief attributes to them, that the architectural concepts described in agent files on disk match the brief's claims, and that the revision cycles specified in the workflow will be sufficient to catch any prose defects. Each of these is a presupposition subject to challenge. A CAUSAL_CLAIM presupposition that "the agent-updater workflow described in the brief will produce consistent documentation" can be tested against the actual workflow sequence in the orchestrator's agent file. A DATA_STATE presupposition that "bibliography.bib contains entries for every citation key used in the brief" can be tested directly. The adversarial agent performs these tests, reports which presuppositions are validated and which must be addressed, and returns a recommendation (PROCEED, PROCEED WITH CAUTION, REVISE BEFORE PROCEEDING, or HALT) before any drafting activity begins.

4. Cascade Analysis and Dependency Tracing

The governance value of presupposition critique depends on what follows from a challenged assumption: which inferences, decisions, and actions depend on an expectation that may not hold. For each challenged presupposition, the adversarial performs cascade analysis. It traces the dependency chain through the plan's logic to its terminal actions and outcomes, annotating each node with a survival status.

The four survival annotations are: SURVIVES (the downstream conclusion holds even if the presupposition is replaced by its alternative), WEAKENED (the conclusion is less certain but remains possibly valid), INVALIDATED (the conclusion does not hold and the plan must be revised), and REDIRECTED (the conclusion changes direction but remains actionable). The analysis produces a dependency tree whose structure reveals the plan's vulnerability profile. A deeply branching cascade from a single challenged presupposition, with many INVALIDATED terminal nodes, indicates a plan with a fragile foundation; a shallow cascade with mostly SURVIVES annotations indicates a robust plan whose presupposition failures are locally contained.

Cascade analysis also identifies cross-boundary effects. When a plan affects multiple components of the project, a challenged presupposition may cascade not only through the plan's internal logic but across system, agent, or workflow boundaries. These cross-boundary cascades are explicitly flagged because they represent coordination risks: whether from expectations that are mutually incompatible between agents or from expectations that, though mutually compatible, are jointly invalid because they rest on a false picture of the environment. The plan may be locally coherent but generate inconsistencies in parts of the system that the plan's authors were not attending to. The identification of cross-boundary cascades is the adversarial agent's contribution to the coordination problem Hayek identified. Plans based on incompatible expectations become visible not when the plans are executed and the conflict materializes, but before execution, when the challenge traces the dependency chain through the overlapping domains to its cross-boundary implications.

In this project, a cascade analysis might reveal that a presupposition underlying a chapter-revision plan, that revising §3 will not require updating the chapter's cross-references to other chapters, cascades through the following chain: if §3 is revised to use a different terminology (SCOPE_BOUNDARY presupposition fails), then the chapter's terminology will diverge from the chapters that reference it (cross-boundary cascade), triggering a series of Terminology Mismatch entries in the conflict log when the conflict auditor runs (Workflow 4 closure). The cascade is INVALIDATED at the scope assumption node and REDIRECTED at the remediation node (the plan still proceeds, but with a broader revision scope than originally assumed). The adversarial agent reports this cascade before the revision begins, allowing the orchestrator to adjust the workflow's scope rather than discovering the terminology drift after the conflict auditor's report.

5. The Mandatory Opener Pattern

Chapter 9 established the mandatory closer: every content-creating and content-modifying workflow ends with a conflict audit. The closer's function is reactive: it detects inconsistencies that the workflow introduced. The mandatory opener introduced by the adversarial agent is its proactive complement. Before a plan is executed, the adversarial agent reviews the plan's presuppositions. The governance architecture brackets production between these two patterns. Workflows open with presupposition critique and close with consistency verification.

The bracket is not symmetric in function. The closer operates on the project's state after changes have been made. It reads the modified files and checks them for consistency. The opener operates on the plan before changes are made. It reads the plan's structure and tests the conditions it assumes. The closer addresses what went wrong: inconsistencies introduced between the modified outputs and the rest of the project. The opener addresses what might go wrong: expectations that are mutually incompatible or that do not correspond to the actual state of the environment. Each catches a class of problems the other cannot see.

The mandatory opener pattern applies to workflows where a plan is executed that could have been based on mistaken presuppositions: chapter drafting (Workflow 3), chapter revision (Workflow 4), and cleanup operations (Workflow 8). It does not apply to procedural workflows with fully specified steps and no substantive judgment, because those workflows do not rest on presuppositions about the project's state in the same way. The distinction is between workflows that rest on expectations about what the environment contains, what agents will do, and what causal relationships hold (expectations that may be both mutually incompatible and externally invalid) and workflows where the agent executes a predetermined procedure that does not require such expectations to succeed.

In this project, the orchestrator's constitutional rules were revised when the adversarial agent was added to the team: "Always close with @conflict-auditor — after any multi-file change session." The opener rule is embedded in the workflow specifications for Workflows 3, 4, and 8: the adversarial agent is invoked after the chapter expert prepares a Chapter Brief (Workflow 3), after the book-content-expert prepares a revision plan (Workflow 4), and after the code-hygiene agent identifies deletion candidates (Workflow 8). In each case, the adversarial reviews the plan's presuppositions before any execution begins. The governance bracket is: open with adversarial critique, execute the production or modification workflow, close with conflict-auditor verification. The adversarial agent's position is visible in the workflow diagrams for Workflow 3 (Chapter Drafting) and Workflow 4 (Chapter Revision), where it appears immediately after the planning step and before production begins. In Workflow 8 (Cleanup), the same mandatory opener pattern applies to deletion operations.

The bracket also reflects the temporal asymmetry between proactive and reactive governance. The adversarial agent's findings can be acted upon at zero cost. If challenge reveals a presupposition that does not hold, the plan can be revised before any files are modified. The conflict auditor's findings require remediation after the fact. The inconsistency has been introduced, and correcting it requires modifying files that are already in the project. The mandatory opener trades a small up-front analytical cost for a potentially large reduction in remediation cost. The mandatory closer catches what the opener missed or what arose from the execution itself, completing the coverage.

6. Read-Only Critique and the Separation of Analysis from Action

Chapter 10 noted that the two-agent structure of detection and adjudication makes the quality-assurance process adversarial in a constructive sense. The auditor's findings must survive the resolution agent's evaluation, and the resolution agent's dispositions must be justified against the authority hierarchy's explicit criteria. The adversarial agent gives institutional form to a broader version of that principle. Its role is to play the critic, not the decider. It surfaces what was assumed and tests whether the assumptions hold. It does not modify files, execute plans, or resolve the presuppositions it challenges. Everything it finds is returned to the orchestrator for routing.

The read-only constraint serves the same function here that it serves in the security and conflict-auditing agents: it separates the analytical function from the action function. If the adversarial could modify plans, its cascade analysis could restructure the plan in ways that introduce new presuppositions, and the object of examination would shift under analysis. The adversarial must examine the expectations the architecture has facilitated as they are, not as they would be after its own intervention. All modifications flow through the orchestrator and the relevant production agents, keeping the critique unentangled from its subject.

The adversarial agent's recommendations are advisory. It returns one of four verdicts: PROCEED, PROCEED WITH CAUTION, REVISE BEFORE PROCEEDING, or HALT. Only the HALT verdict signals that the plan has fundamental instability. The other verdicts are graduated. PROCEED WITH CAUTION indicates presuppositions that are not fully validated but are not obviously false; REVISE BEFORE PROCEEDING indicates specific challenged presuppositions that must be addressed. In all cases, the orchestrator (informed by the adversarial agent's findings) decides whether to proceed. The adversarial agent strengthens the basis for that decision; it does not make the decision itself.

This advisory role is the institutional complement of the security agent's absolute veto. The security agent operates where the stakes of proceeding are categorical and constitutional. Credentials must never be exposed, destructive operations require clearance. In those domains, a veto is appropriate because no advisory recommendation would be sufficient. I chose an advisory model for the adversarial agent because its domain is different. External validity, unlike consistency, admits of degree. An expectation may be well-grounded, partially supported, or entirely unfounded. The graduated verdicts are a function of inferred validity. The plan's authors are in a position to weigh the findings and respond. Together, the adversarial and security agents cover the governance space from analytical critique to constitutional enforcement, each operating in the mode appropriate to its domain.
