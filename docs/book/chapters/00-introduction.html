1. Why Write this Book?

I am not technically a computer scientist. I am trained as a computational economist. I prefer my agents to be naive and to learn together from experience. For years, this made me feel like a dinosaur, as computational economics led many of my colleagues toward computational macroeconomics of the DSGE variety and computational finance. In my career, I have done my best to maintain awareness of developments in more advanced forms of reinforcement learning, but I maintained my focus on theories of organization, especially those promoted from within transaction cost economics.

Organizations exist to reduce transaction costs. When I wrote my thesis, I presented my findings as a means of understanding the reduction of search costs. The more important finding was that economies of naive agents are self-organizing in a manner far beyond the zero-intelligence trading model of Gode and Sunder. I had always planned to follow up on these findings, but I was too busy concerning myself with the fundamentals of organization at varying scales, from teams and communities to societies and transregional economies.

It is fortunate that I followed my interests, as the new wave of artificial intelligence is greatly enriched by abstract theories of organizations and societies and the constitutions that hold them together. Constitutions operate at all analytical levels. Each actor can be conceived of as embodying a constitution. Likewise, cooperating agents rely on constitutional scaffolding, even if that scaffolding is mostly implicit.

In order to function efficiently, agents that form agent teams need constitutional scaffolding. And the agent teams themselves require constitutional scaffolding. What is the responsibility and understanding of each agent? By what interface do agents cooperate? How are duties and rights defined for each agent? How are conflicts resolved? How can constitutions adjust to allow a team to adapt to changing circumstances and knowledge without compromising the constitutional system itself?

A well-ordered response to these questions requires significant abstraction from any particular team that is assigned to a particular task or project. Add to this the challenge that the growing knowledge of each particular agent team must be integrated into an abstract constitutional structure in order to enable constructive adaptation. Essentially, our task is to facilitate the development of a team that acts as a self-sustaining and self-authoring system.

This task requires more than computer science. It is an interdisciplinary problem. A proper response to this challenge requires a theory of action that integrates agent understanding. It requires a theory of governance that scales across levels of analysis, from small teams and families to collections of organizations.

2. A Brief Description of the Approach

What is a coding agent? We can define the coding agent as the bundle of a large language model with infrastructure to efficaciously guide that model's activity toward productive ends. But to understand how to use the agent, we must borrow from theories of economics, entrepreneurship, and organization. Coase (1937) and Williamson (1985) identified the characteristic features of the firm: a defined authority structure, a division of labor, and a governance regime that coordinates activity. An agent or agent team is best understood, on this account, as an organization whose members exercise means-ends rationality when allocated judgment, ranking outcomes and evaluating means within an environment that responds to their actions. The rationality is functional rather than phenomenological; Mises (1949) would recognize the pattern as purposeful behavior in his sense. What distinguishes a productive team from an unproductive one is less a matter of raw model capability than of infrastructure quality: the specification files, authority hierarchies, and governance procedures that shape the preference ordering within which judgment operates.

Drawing on Hayek, Mises, Menger, Kirzner, and Lachmann for the analysis of dispersed knowledge and spontaneous order; on Coase, Williamson, and Foss and Klein for the theory of the firm; on North and Ostrom for institutional analysis; and on Nonaka for the dynamics of organizational knowledge creation, the book develops this argument from the elementary architecture of a single coding agent through the constitutional constraints and division of labor that comprise well-functioning multi-agent teams, to the individual agents that instantiate specialized roles and the mechanisms through which team and infrastructure maintain quality and co-evolve.

Early draft. This manuscript is incomplete and subject to substantial revision. It is an experiment in AI-assisted academic authorship: written in active cooperation with an agent team, with the author directing the research agenda, theoretical arguments, and editorial decisions while agents assisted with drafting, revision, and consistency auditing.
