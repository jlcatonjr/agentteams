Chapter 2: The Problem of Context

Part I: Foundations

1. The Long-Short-Term Memory Problem

In "The Use of Knowledge in Society," Hayek (1945, 519–520) argued that the economic problem of society is not the allocation of given resources by a rational central planner but the utilization of knowledge dispersed across many actors, each of whom possesses "knowledge of the particular circumstances of time and place" that no central authority can aggregate. The man on the spot knows something that the planner does not. The solution, Hayek argued, is not to transmit all knowledge to a central node but to allow the naturally emergent price system to serve its role as a communication system. Prices convey compressed signals sufficient for the local actor to decide and act well. Prices are, thus, knowledge aggregates.

The price system addresses a practical problem: transmitting dispersed local knowledge, but it requires something that Hayek's earlier paper, "Economics and Knowledge" (1937), had shown cannot be taken for granted. The 1937 paper asks a logically prior question: under what conditions do the plans of independent actors become mutually compatible? Formal equilibrium theory, Hayek argued, is tautological on this point. It defines equilibrium as the state in which plans are compatible but does not explain how agents acquire the knowledge that makes their expectations correct. That explanation requires empirical content that the pure logic of choice cannot supply. The coding agent's context window forces both of these problems upon the architect simultaneously: the agent cannot hold all project knowledge in mind, so it needs compressed signals. But for those signals to work, the agents reading them must share the expectations, conventions, and interpretive context that give compressed data its meaning.

1.1 The Empirical Phenomenon

As a conversation between a user and a coding agent grows, the agent will likely begin to lose track of its earliest instructions. Project conventions that had been reliably followed are overridden by more recent and often lower-priority context. The prime directives (the project conventions, authority hierarchy, constitutional constraints) sit at the top of the context window but recede in effective weight as the token count grows. What began as firm guidance becomes a faint signal beneath accumulated conversation.

A multi-agent project orchestrator that reliably routes work to the correct domain agent in a short session may, after extensive dialogue involving multiple rounds of drafting, revision, and cross-referencing, begin to conflate domain agents, skip security checks, or apply workflow steps out of order even when earlier instructions are still present in the context window. Their effective weight relative to the surrounding mass of recent conversation diminishes.

1.2 Beyond Token Limits

Token limits will expand with each model generation. But the problem described above is not primarily one of window size. The self-attention mechanism of the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al. 2017) allocates finite computational weight across every token in the context. Even with longer windows, the agent must prioritize instructions. It cannot attend equally to every instruction at every moment. This is an allocation problem. There exists a scarcity of interpretive attention that must be economized.

The deeper problem is interpretive. The agent needs a frame within which data becomes meaningful. The project-level instructions, authority hierarchy, voice samples, and constitutional rules that form the agent’s interpretive context are frames through which new data is interpreted. When that context degrades under token pressure, what erodes first is not factual content but the orderliness of the composite frame that tells the agent how to read and respond to new content. An agent who has lost track of the authority hierarchy interprets signals inconsistently. The result is the familiar cascade of scope drift, conflation, and misapplied procedures.

1.3 Working Memory

Miller (1956) described the limits of human working memory as "the magical number seven, plus or minus two." The total stock of human knowledge vastly exceeds what can be brought to bear on any particular task. Productive capacity depends not on the total stock but on how much can be activated in the context of a specific decision.

Agent productivity depends on the quality of what is active in its context and on the agent's interpretive frame. This dependence has a further implications. Interpretation is not a passive reception of signals but an active operation on them, one that can prompt the agent to reorganize which elements of its context receive priority and to act on what the reorganized frame reveals (Clark 2016). When the frame degrades, the first casualty is the agent's capacity to read that data correctly. A persistent challenge that agent architecture must meet head-on is maintenance of interpretive coherence that no growing context window can indefinitely alleviate.

2. The Man on the Spot and the Communication Problem

Each agent in a multi-agent team is a man on the spot for its domain. For our project, the bibliography manager knows citation conventions, verification procedures, and the safeguards that prevent fabricated references. The voice-style agent knows the principal's published prose patterns, voice features calibrated from those patterns, and the deviation-detection protocols that maintain stylistic consistency. The LaTeX specialist knows manuscript compilation, cross-referencing conventions, and the BibTeX pipeline that connects citations to the bibliography database. No single agent holds all of this knowledge simultaneously. The context window is what makes each agent local. It prevents any single agent from becoming a central planner over the whole project and creates the demand for organizational structure.

2.1 Knowledge Proxies

Hayek's solution to the knowledge problem was to build a communication system that transmits knowledge proxies: compressed signals carrying enough information for the local actor to decide and act well. A rising price does not explain the drought, the trade policy, or the supply disruption that caused the shortage. It conveys enough to guide action: "economize on this resource, seek substitutes" (Hayek 1945, 527). In the agent system, documentation files, navigation documents, and summary reports serve the same function. A navigation document tells the agent where to look rather than what to know. A chapter outline transmits argument structure without reproducing full text. A stage report transmits process results without reproducing logs. These are compressed signals designed to fit the context window and guide action without loading full underlying detail.

The quality of the communication system determines the quality of coordination across the team. A proxy that is too compressed loses essential information. Conversely, one that is too detailed defeats its own purpose by consuming the context it was designed to economize. The design problem is the same one Hayek identified for the price system: how to encode enough information to guide local action without overwhelming the local actor's capacity to process it.

2.2 The Interpretive Frame

Knowledge proxies, however, are not self-interpreting. Hayek (1937) showed in "Economics and Knowledge" that the compatibility of agents' expectations is an empirical condition, not a logical guarantee. Formal equilibrium theory assumes that agents hold correct expectations but cannot explain how they come to do so. The implication for communication systems is direct: the data available to each agent, which includes prices, quantities, and observable behavior, are not sufficient by themselves. What makes these data informative is the shared context of expectations, conventions, and institutional structure within which each agent reads the signals available to him. A price signal is useless to an actor who does not understand the conventions of the market in which it appears. The interpretive frame is what converts data into knowledge, and its existence is the empirical proposition that Hayek's 1937 analysis identified as the foundation of coordination.

For agent teams, the interpretive frame is formed by the project-level instructions, the authority hierarchy, the voice samples, the constitutional rules, and the .agent.md files that define each agent's role. These documents are not knowledge proxies. They do not transmit compressed signals within an already-constituted interpretive context. They form that context. A navigation document is a knowledge proxy, a compressed signal that an agent reads within the frame its agent file provides. The agent file itself is part of the frame: it tells the agent how to read the navigation document, what to attend to, what to disregard.

The distinction between signals read within a frame and the frame itself is functional. The same document might serve as a proxy in one context and as a frame component in another. But the distinction matters for understanding degradation. When the context window fills and the agent's understanding its documentation loosens, what erodes first is the frame. An agent that has lost its interpretive frame interprets differently, and the result is systematic: scope drift, conflation, procedure violations, and the characteristic failure mode in which the agent confidently applies the wrong workflow to the right problem.

A comment on terminology: not all structured documents in an agent team are knowledge proxies, and not all are components of the interpretive frame. Agent files (.agent.md files that define role, domain knowledge, and workflow) are frame components. Navigation files, stage reports, change-detection logs, and chapter briefs are knowledge proxies. This boundary will need to be respected when the automated governance pipeline (Chapters 17–19) introduces new document types.

The creation of knowledge proxies, which includes navigation documents, stage reports, chapter briefs, is what Nonaka (1994) calls externalization: the conversion of tacit domain understanding into explicit, transmittable form. The agent files that form the interpretive frame serve as what Nonaka and Konno (1998) term ba, shared contexts within which those proxies acquire meaning. The individual agent remains, as Nonaka insists, the prime mover in organizational knowledge creation: it is the local agent's capacity to interpret proxies within its frame that converts compressed signals into productive action. The organizational architecture distributes knowledge without replacing the individual act of interpretation.

2.3 The Knowledge That Cannot Be Centralized

Hayek's argument against central planning rests not on the claim that the planner lacks computing power but on the claim that the nature of the relevant knowledge resists centralization (Butos and McQuade 2002). The knowledge is dispersed across many actors. It is tacit, embedded in practice and skill, not fully articulable in propositions. And it acquires its relevance and meaning only in the circumstances of a specific decision.

The agent team faces a comparable problem. The total knowledge of a project (all source files, all git history, all stakeholder preferences, all intermediate build states) cannot be loaded into a single context window. Even if it could, the agent would still need to prioritize, and the interpretive frame required to make the data meaningful is itself a product of institutional design, of agent files and conventions and authority hierarchies, not of storage capacity. Increasing the storage capacity without improving the interpretive frame is analogous to giving the central planner a larger desk. The desk was never the binding constraint.

Context windows will grow with each model generation, eventually dissolving the constraint — or so it seems. The failure of this reasoning is illuminating. Hayek's argument was not that the planner's desk was too small. His argument was that the nature of the knowledge resisted the kind of aggregation central planning requires, because some knowledge is tacit, situational, and only emerges in the context of action. Some project knowledge likewise only becomes relevant in the context of a specific task and cannot be usefully pre-stated (O'Driscoll and Rizzo 1985). Some knowledge is generated by the act of investigation itself, discovered in the course of engaging with a problem rather than retrieved from a pre-compiled archive. And the interpretive frame that gives data its meaning is not a datum to be stored but an institutional structure to be maintained. Larger context windows relax the quantitative constraint, which is welcome, but they do not dissolve the qualitative problem of interpretive organization.

The conversation between agent and environment, however, runs in both directions. When an agent acts, which includes writes code, updates documentation, commits a change, it does not execute within an unchanged environment. Its output modifies the shared project state that the next agent will encounter and interpret. The man on the spot who acts on his local knowledge alters the circumstances of time and place that make up the knowledge available to his successors. In an agent team, every file produced, every documentation update issued, every structural change committed transforms the environment from which subsequent agents will form their interpretations. Coordination thus requires more than that each agent interpret its own proxies well. It requires that agents act with awareness that their outputs are themselves environmental signals, feeding back into the interpretive context of the agents that follow. The relationship between agents and their shared environment is one of ongoing exchange. Each round of interpretation shapes the action that reshapes the environment that shapes the next round of interpretation.

3. The Degradation Curve

Context degradation follows a characteristic pattern. Performance is stable in short sessions. As the context fills, performance degrades gradually: minor deviations appear, lower-priority conventions are occasionally violated, the agent begins to favor recent context over foundational instructions. Once a threshold is crossed (a threshold that varies by model and by the complexity of the instructions), performance drops sharply. The agent's awareness on the organizational structure degrades and begins to produce output that is locally coherent but globally inconsistent. Common patterns include scope drift and nonsense deletions of core scripts.

3.1 Signal Distortion

With context overload, agent interpretation becomes progressively less coherent as context fills. The distortion is systematic: the agent increasingly follows recent context in a manner not aligned with foundational instructions. The agent fails to prioritize its interpretive frame. Conventions, priorities, and authority hierarchy that told the agent how to read those signals erode.

Because degradation follows a pattern, it can be designed around. The strategies developed below, and the organizational structures developed throughout the book, are responses to a predictable constraint. The agent team's architecture is a response to the context constraint, shaped by the same logic that shapes market institutions as a response to the knowledge problem. The context window creates the demand for organizational structure, and the organizational structure economizes on the scarce resource. Every design decision in the chapters that follow can be traced to this foundation.

4. Building the Communication System

Well-written documentation compresses project knowledge into knowledge proxies that transmit enough information for the agent receiving direction to pursue its task. The compression is not lossless. Some information is discarded. The measure of a good proxy is adequacy: does it carry enough information, in sufficiently compact form, for the agent to functionally pursue its assigned task? A good knowledge proxy will provide agent knowledge of what to do and where to look for additional information. Navigation documents, for example, tell the agent where to look rather than what to know. A navigator file does not contain the content of every project file. It contains a map of where that content lives, organized by domain and function. The agent consults the map, identifies the relevant source, and reads only what it needs for the current task.

4.1 Modular Decomposition

A standard feature of any quality library used in software development is modularity. This is, in fact, part of the Unix philosophy: "Write programs that do one thing and do it well. Write programs to work together." Modularity is a response to the problem of context. (Thank you to Chris Cowden for bringing this to my attention). Koppl and Langlois (2001) show that the same logic governs firm boundaries: when knowledge is specialized and interdependencies are high, organizations decompose into modules that minimize the cognitive load on any single decision-maker. Each module handles its own domain, and interfaces between modules are narrow and well-defined. Agent teams follow this architecture for the same reason. So we should not be surprised that agent teams should themselves follow similar principles of modularity. Breaking large documentation files into smaller, specialized components distributes knowledge to where it is needed. Each agent loads only its own .agent.md file plus the project-level instructions. This special background allows the agent to uniquely pursue tasks and extract particular knowledge and information from the environment in a manner not possible, at least not as efficiently and systematically, by other agents.

Long inline procedures within agent files (multi-step workflows, validation sequences, audit checklists) also consume context without contributing to the agent's interpretive frame. They are instructions for execution, not components of the understanding that guides execution. Refactoring these into standalone scripts that the agent file merely references reorganizes the same productive capacity into a form that economizes on the scarce resource. The agent file retains the reference. The script's content enters the context only when the procedure is needed, a reorganization whose capital-theoretic significance Chapter 3 develops.

It should be clear why using a monolithic instructions file that contains all project conventions, all agent definitions, and all workflow procedures is a terrible idea. This approach attempts to centralize control and will quickly run up against computational limits. Even if it fits within the context window, the volume of information would dilute the effective weight of any single instruction. The distributed architecture puts each agent in possession of the knowledge and knowledge proxies relevant to his particular circumstances and provides each of them different potentials.

4.2 Living Document Policy: Adapting Interpretation

The living document policy is a commitment to keeping all documentation current and removing stale content. This serves both the transmission of accurate knowledge proxies and the integrity of the interpretive frame. A stale agent file constitutes an incorrect interpretive context, not merely transmitting outdated information but systematically skewing the agent's reading of every subsequent signal.

We will continue developing this concept. For now, it is sufficient to recognize that every system must maintain vigilance against two threats: obsolescence and drift. The living document policy is a commitment to renewal justified by changing circumstances. Yet, we must also delineate which conventions are subject to change, which are not, and to what extent particular conventions can tolerate drift.

4.3 Abstractions as Knowledge Proxies

A good theoretical abstraction captures the essential features of a phenomenon while discarding irrelevant detail: compressing reality into a form the theorist's mind can hold and manipulate. A good code abstraction (a well-designed class, a clean API) does the same, encapsulating complexity behind a simple interface. Agent documentation abstractions compress project knowledge in the same way. A chapter outline abstracts argument structure. A theory map abstracts the relationship between architectural concepts and theoretical traditions. A citation tracker abstracts the relationship between keys and chapters, relying on a structure that enables systematic mapping. Each discards detail irrelevant to the current task while preserving relevant details.

Not all knowledge proxies are equally good. A poorly written agent file may consume context without conveying actionable information. The quality of a proxy can be assessed by two criteria: compression efficiency (how much useful knowledge per token?) and interpretive clarity (can the proxy be correctly interpreted within the agent's frame?). A maximally compressed but cryptic proxy may be efficient for security but excessively cumbersome for any other purpose.

4.4 The Bridge to Capital Theory

If knowledge proxies are the mechanism by which the man on the spot receives the knowledge he needs, and if building effective proxies requires investment (time, effort, iterative refinement), then these proxies are productive resources. Their creation requires investment of time and effort and are subject to opportunity costs. With this in mind, developers must view the creation and maintenance of this architecture as an integral part of their enterprise. As knowledge depreciates (consider a navigation document not updated to reflect recent structural changes), that depreciation will be associated with rising development costs. The depreciating capital requires investment to restore and, potentially, improve beyond its initial state. Likewise, this bundle of capital is full of complementarities. A navigation document is useless without the files it references. A citation tracker is useless without the bibliography database it indexes. An agent file is useless without the project-level instructions that form the broader interpretive frame. An orchestrator agent will struggle to provide clear instructions if it cannot reference the mapping provided by the navigator agent.

We have seen that the context constraint creates the demand for abstractions. This parallels knowledge constraints of individual humans, constraints that drive the development of living organizations. The abstractions that are integral to our agent teams are productive resources with the properties of capital goods. The communication systems described in this chapter require continual reinvestment. These structures depreciate without maintenance. Without them, project plan formation and implementation become dysfunctional.

Sustaining the agent infrastructure is akin to maintaining the integrity of a firm's organizational structure. Good infrastructure supports the ongoing knowledge spiral process required for adaptive organizations. Quality interpretation is required to order action, as well as reorganization of the existing infrastructure. Interpretation, action, and reorganization are steps that feed back on one another.
