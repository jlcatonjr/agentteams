Chapter 22: Agent Teams as Spontaneous Orders

Part VI: Synthesis

1. Designed Systems with Emergent Properties

Everything described in this book was designed in cooperation with a large language model. Without the human principal, the book would not exist. Yet, without his employment of AI to co-participate in this project, the ideas developed in this book would have remained a kernel of a blog post. The orchestrator, the constitutional constraints, the investigation protocol, the daily pipeline, the living document policy: a human principal created all of it. And yet the system exhibits properties that no one designed. Multiple repositories independently developed similar governance patterns (Chapter 17): Mengerian institutional formation, where common structures arise from independent action under similar constraints. The investigator discovered patterns the system's architects did not anticipate (Chapter 18): Kirznerian entrepreneurial alertness, the capacity to notice what others have overlooked (Koppl et al. 2015). The investigation-remediation cycle corrects problems without human intervention for routine governance (Chapters 20–21), and coherent coordination emerges from distributed rule-following without any single agent holding a global view (Chapter 19): Hayekian rule-governed coordination (Lewis 2012; Lewis 2016; Lewis 2017). How can designed systems produce emergent, well-ordered properties without any individual intention to produce them (Holland 1999)? The question is the central problem of institutional theory, and Hayek devoted his career to its resolution (Hayek 1974). Axtell (2016) demonstrates that agent-based computation and Hayekian spontaneous order theory are mutually enriching: complexity science supplies the formal tools for characterizing the emergent patterns that Austrian theory identifies. Wagner (2020) frames the complementary macro-level question: how does system-level order emerge from distributed rule-following without any agent designing the aggregate outcome?

2. The Cosmos/Taxis Spectrum

In Law, Legislation and Liberty (1973), Hayek distinguished between two types of order. Cosmos is spontaneous order: order that arises from agents following general rules without anyone planning the overall pattern. Language, law, money, and markets are cosmos. They arise from human action but not from human design. Taxis is designed order: order that results from deliberate arrangement according to a plan. A factory floor, a military formation, a piece of legislation is taxis. The designer specifies the arrangement. Hayek argued that the great error of rationalist social theory was to assume that all order must be taxis, that if something exhibits order, someone must have ordered it.

Agent teams occupy a position on the spectrum between these poles that is theoretically interesting. They are designed in their constitutional structure. The invariant cores, the authority hierarchy, the workflow definitions, the pipeline stages are all deliberate creations. The developer who creates the agent team exercises the entrepreneurial judgment that Foss and Klein (2012) describe: choosing conventions, structuring authority, organizing the team's capital into a complementary structure (Chapter 3). As the team's infrastructure develops, agents allocated judgment extend this function (instantiating specialists from templates, propagating governance updates) so that the capacity to generate organizational infrastructure is itself a product of the organizational capital already in place (Chapter 1). This is taxis.

But agent teams are emergent in their behavioral coordination. The convergent patterns across repositories, the investigation discoveries, the self-correcting governance loop arise from agents following local rules within the communication system (Chapter 2) without any agent planning the aggregate outcome. This is cosmos. The design creates conditions under which the emergence occurs. The module's reusable template library is one archive of this dynamic: I identified governance patterns that emerged independently across repositories (Chapter 17), abstracted them into structural templates, and made them portable — convergent informal practice converted into transferable institutional form. The cosmos/taxis distinction that licenses this framing, as Hayek formalized it, traces to the Greek philosophers and was revived by the Scottish Enlightenment. Ferguson (1767) captured it: social institutions are "the result of human action, but not the execution of any human design." The agent team is a 21st-century instantiation of this classical insight (Menger 1892).

The agent-team case suggests that the cosmos/taxis distinction is better understood as a spectrum, and that the most productive institutional arrangements may be those that deliberately design the conditions for spontaneous coordination. Ostrom (1990) arrived at a similar conclusion through her analysis of commons governance: the most successful communities were those that designed rules enabling self-organizing cooperation, not those that relied on either pure central control or pure laissez-faire. The agent team provides an unusually clean illustration because its institutions are explicit code, its agents' institutional constraints directly readable in the source files, and its coordination observable and measurable. It is, in a sense, a laboratory for institutional theory. The variables that social scientists must infer from survey data and field observation are here directly readable in the source files.

3. The Information Problem Revisited

The thread running through every chapter has been the two-part problem Hayek identified. Hayek (1937) showed that the compatibility of individual plans is an empirical condition that formal equilibrium theory assumes but cannot explain: agents must acquire the knowledge that makes their expectations correct, and the pure logic of choice does not account for how they do so. Hayek (1945) then described one institutional response to this problem: the price system as a communication mechanism that transmits dispersed local knowledge through compressed signals. The agent-team's documentation system serves an analogous function. Both are information media that compress distributed knowledge into actionable signals (what Chapter 2 calls knowledge proxies). Both enable decentralized coordination: no single actor needs to know everything. Each acts on the proxies available at his location. Both require an interpretive frame to function: a price signal is meaningless to an actor who does not understand the market's conventions. And both are subject to degradation: inflation corrupts prices. Documentation drift corrupts instructions. In both cases, what degrades first is the interpretive frame that makes the signals meaningful.

The parallel is real but not unlimited. Prices are generated by voluntary exchange under scarcity. Documentation is generated by directed production under resource constraints. Prices adjust through competitive market processes. Documentation adjusts through designed maintenance cycles. Prices carry information about subjective valuations across the economy. Documentation carries information about system structure across the team. Chapter 2's concept of the communication system captures what is common to both: architectures for transmitting knowledge proxies between distributed actors who each hold only local knowledge. The mechanism-level differences mark where the analogy ends.

Caton and Lopez (2020), drawing on Douglass North's institutional analysis, argue that institutions have a cognitive dimension: they structure cognition, shaping how agents perceive, categorize, and process information (North 2005). Agent documentation is a direct instantiation of this cognitive dimension. An agent file forms the cognitive environment within which the agent processes information and produces output. The orchestrator's domain routing table is a cognitive map that determines which aspects of the project the orchestrator attends to and which it delegates. The practical concern of the book (how to design effective agent teams) and the deeper theoretical question (how do institutions shape cognition and coordination?) meet at this point. Documentation is cognitive infrastructure: the institutional substrate through which agents process the world they act upon. The connection to Chapter 3's embedded technology is direct. The technology embedded in capital goods (Chapter 3) is cognitive infrastructure. These are two perspectives on the same phenomenon. Chapter 3 shows that the technology exists in the files. Caton and Lopez show that those files form the cognitive environment within which agents process and act.

4. Limitations of the Analogy

The economic analogy has been productive throughout, but its limits should be stated plainly. Chapter 1 established that agents exercise means-ends rationality when allocated judgment, ranking outcomes, evaluating means, selecting among alternatives through composite preference orderings shaped by model weights, system instructions, and environmental context. What agents lack is not rationality but market institutions. Agents do not own property. There are no resource rights that agents can trade or defend. Agents bear no costs of their own. A failed API call costs the human principal, not the agent. Agents are assigned roles, not selected by competitive pressure. The phenomenological disclaimer still holds: agents do not experience preference in the way human actors do. But functionally, their behavior is purposeful, rational action in the Misesian (1949) sense, and the institutional insights the book develops apply to rational agents operating within designed governance structures, not to mere instruction-followers.

Miller and Drexler (1988a; 1988b; 1988c) described "agoric open systems," computational architectures where software agents operate in actual computational markets with resource constraints, bidding mechanisms, and property rights. In an agoric system, agents would own computational resources (memory, CPU time, API calls), bid for access to shared resources, and bear the cost of wasteful operations. Such a system would close the gap between the economic analogy and the computational reality. Agents in an agoric system would compete, economize, and respond to prices.

Current agent teams are not agoric. Resource allocation is centralized (the human principal pays for API calls). Agents do not bid for resources. There is no price mechanism. The economic analogy holds at the institutional level (rules, governance, constitutional constraints, information processing) but not at the market level (prices, competition, property rights).

The gap can be stated with some precision. A Hayekian agent-based economy requires, at minimum: agents with defined purposes, a mechanism for attributing good or bad status to outcomes, ongoing valuation, experimentation that generates novel information, and an evolutionary selection mechanism that retains successful configurations (Caton 2025). The agent team has most of these. Agents have defined purposes (their mandate files), and good-status attribution is institutionalized across multiple auditing agents: the code-hygiene agent, the conflict auditor, and the voice-style agent each assess outputs against defined standards. Experimentation occurs through open-ended investigation protocols that surface patterns no prior specification anticipated. A rudimentary evolutionary selection operates at the abstract layer, where patterns proven effective in individual repositories are identified, abstracted, and propagated system-wide. What the team lacks is the market-mechanism subset: property rights over computational resources, ongoing valuation through pricing, and exchange through bidding. The agent team is an organizational economy without a price system, the institutional layer that, in a Hayekian account, would precede the emergence of market coordination.

One might argue that the absence of market mechanisms makes the entire economic framing inappropriate. But this overstates the case. The institutional insights, that distributed systems require constitutional rules, that information media require consistency, that coordination under partial knowledge requires structured communication, hold regardless of whether agents bear costs of this kind. The economic framework illuminates the institutional structure of agent teams. The market framework awaits more sophisticated architectures. The limitation is empirical. If future agent architectures incorporate actual resource constraints and bidding mechanisms, the analogy would extend further.

A different and more fundamental limitation concerns the empirical base of the book's claims. Everything described here was drawn from a single project: one author, one manuscript, one team of agents configured and operated over the course of one working year. The governance patterns, the convergent emergence across repositories, the investigation discoveries are observations from a single instance, and one in which the designer, the operator, and the analyst are the same person. Principles derived from a single case cannot be validated against competing instances. Patterns that emerged from this team's development may reflect idiosyncratic choices rather than structural necessities. The framework is a theoretical interpretation of this evidence, not an empirical result generalizable across all agent teams without qualification. Hayek (1955) argued that complex phenomena admit only degrees of explanation: the theorist can identify structural mechanisms and predict their general character, but cannot derive precise outcomes from them. The framework developed here is explanation of that kind. Its value is interpretive: it makes the single instance intelligible through concepts with independent theoretical grounding. Its broader applicability awaits the accumulation of practice that a wider body of agent teams would supply.

5. Future Directions

As LLM costs decline and agent capabilities expand, the next architectural frontier is real resource constraints: agents with budgets who must economize on API calls, agents that compete for computational time, agents that bear the cost of their own mistakes. Miller and Drexler's agoric architecture provides the theoretical blueprint: encapsulated bidding, resource markets, property rights for computational agents. Baum (1999) developed the complementary model of intelligence itself as an economy of sub-agents competing for resources and cooperating to produce integrated behavior — a micro-foundation for the agoric architecture's vision of computational markets. When agents bear the costs of their operations, the economic analogy ceases to be an analogy and becomes a description (Gode and Sunder 1993).

Caton (2017) demonstrated that agent-based computational models can exhibit the entrepreneurial dynamics of discovery under search costs and bounded rationality. Johnson et al. (2025) extend the connection: if the market mechanism is itself a distributed genetic algorithm, then agent teams that encode search, selection, and retention procedures instantiate computational market processes, not merely institutional analogies to them. The present book extends this by showing that real working agent teams, not simulations, exhibit institutional dynamics parallel to those described by Austrian economics (Lavoie, Baetjer, and Tulloh 1990; Boettke and Veetil 2016). The next step is to ask whether working agent teams can be designed so that their institutional dynamics are constituent parts of economic processes. Can agents discover, compete, and coordinate through market mechanisms, not just through designed institutional rules? This is the research frontier toward which the theoretical framework developed here points.

6. From Formula to Theory

This book began with a practical problem: how to design effective agent teams for software development. The existing literature offered formulas. Use an orchestrator. Separate concerns. Maintain documentation. These formulas work, but they are opaque. They do not explain why they work, when they fail, or how to adapt them when conditions change.

I have argued throughout that the theoretical traditions of Austrian economics, institutional political economy, and the theory of spontaneous order provide the explanatory framework these formulas lack. Agent teams face both the epistemological problem of plan compatibility and the practical problem of communicating dispersed knowledge, and their architectural response (distributed documentation, specialized agents, local signals) parallels the market's response. Agent documentation is heterogeneous capital (Lachmann 1956) whose productive technology is embedded in the files themselves and whose structure depends on the plan that organizes them. Maintenance, restructuring, and depreciation follow the dynamics capital theory describes. Agent productivity depends on the alignment of infrastructure with a project’s goals. The developer who initiates this alignment exercises the entrepreneurial judgment Foss and Klein describe. Agents allocated judgment extend it, generating organizational infrastructure as a product of the organizational capital already in place. Agent teams require constitutional rules (Buchanan and Tullock 1962, Ostrom 1990), and the design of those rules follows the logic of institutional political economy. Agent-team patterns emerge spontaneously (Menger 1883), and their codification follows the process by which informal institutions become formal ones.

My own contribution, what separates this book from a mere application of Austrian economics to agent teams, is the recognition that agent documentation functions as cognitive infrastructure in the sense Caton and Lopez (2020) describe. Institutions structure cognition. Agent documentation forms the cognitive environment within which agents process information and produce output. The agent's rationality is inseparable from the infrastructure within which it operates. The convergence links Hayek's knowledge problem (knowledge is distributed and cannot be centralized) with Caton and Lopez's cognitive dimension (how institutions shape agent cognition and coordination). The agent team is a system in which this cognitive-institutional nexus can be observed directly: the documentation is the institution, the institution is the cognitive environment, and the coordination is the emergent product of agents operating within that environment.

Caton (2020b) analyzed Buchanan's social contract theory as a multilevel constitutional framework. Constitutional rules constrain legislative rules, which constrain individual action. The agent team instantiates this multilevel design: invariant cores, authority hierarchies, and the living document policy at the constitutional level. Agent-specific instructions, workflow definitions, and domain routing operate at the legislative level. Individual agent actions within workflows constitute the operational level. Each level constrains the next. The system's stability depends on the integrity of the constitutional level, just as Buchanan argued for political systems. The parallel is not metaphorical. Agent teams are institutional systems that exhibit the same structural properties Buchanan and Tullock analyzed in political constitutions because the problem, coordinating agents with distributed knowledge under constitutional constraints, is the same.

The theoretical framework predicts what agent teams should do. If agent documentation is capital that embodies the technology of its domain, then under-maintenance causes maladaptation. Conflict logs from working teams confirm this. If the context window makes each agent a man on the spot, then distributing knowledge across specialized agents through a well-designed communication system will improve coordination. The functional/domain bifurcation confirms this. If constitutional rules are necessary for team coordination, then teams without them will exhibit coordination failures. The experience of ad hoc prompting versus structured teams confirms this. The theory makes the practice explicable. Practitioners who understand why their practices work are better positioned to adapt when conditions change.

The relationship between theory and practice in this book is reflexive as well as predictive. The theoretical account developed here generated the agentteams module — a codified factory that instantiates teams whose architecture the theory explains (Chapter 17) — and that module generated the agent team which collectively produced this book. Theory begat tool, tool begat team, team begat text: the loop between architectural argument and institutional artifact is not incidental to the book’s demonstration; it is the demonstration.

The book’s argument converges on three commitments stated in Chapter 1. Agents engage in interpretation, not merely execution. Every productive act requires reading the project’s current state, disambiguating context, and deciding which prior instructions apply to this instance. Agents’ actions and their project environment sustain a continuous exchange. Each agent act modifies the environment against which the next agent acts, and the sequence of modifications and responses constitutes the team’s productive history. And agents operate through order-generating mechanisms: the architectural arrangements, which includes the functional/domain division, the constitutional constraints, the communication system, the investigation-remediation cycle, that convert the distributed, partial, tacit knowledge of individual agents into coordinated institutional action that none of the agents individually possesses. These three commitments constitute the theoretically grounded account of agent teams that the existing literature of formulas and checklists cannot provide. Agent teams work for the reasons institutions work. Distributed knowledge, partial information, the need for rules that enable coordination without centralized control: these are the problems, and governance structures built around interpretation, reorganization, and action are the response. The cycle does not terminate. It continues as long as the project and the team that serves it evolve together.
